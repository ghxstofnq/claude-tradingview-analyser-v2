For your Lanto-style playbook, think of **three distinct entry models**: **MSS (Reversal), Trend (Continuation), and Inversion (Failed PD Array)**.
Below are detailed rules for each, plus a concrete **A+ example scenario** so you know exactly what to wait for.

---

## MSS model – Reversal after liquidity grab

**Purpose:** Catch a **reversal** at or near a session high/low or HTF POI after stops are taken and structure shifts.

### Core components

For a **long MSS** (invert for short):

1. **Context & Draw**
    - HTF bias: There is a clear downside draw that is likely near completion (e.g., HTF sell‑side taken, or price is trading into a large 4H/Daily bullish FVG).[4][1]
    - You expect a **reaction higher** once the final liquidity is cleared.
2. **Liquidity Grab**
    - Price runs **below** an obvious low:
        - Asia low, London low, previous day low, or a very clear intra‑day swing low.[5][1]
    - That sweep takes out stops and maybe prints a **long lower wick**.
3. **Market Structure Shift (MSS) with Displacement**
    - After the sweep, price **reverses sharply up** and:
        - Breaks above the most recent **lower high** (internal structure).
        - Does it with **displacement**: a wide‑range candle or series of candles that leave an FVG.[6][1][3]
4. **Retrace to Bullish FVG**
    - The displacement leg creates a **bullish FVG**.
    - Price pulls back into that FVG (ideally the **consequent encroachment** / mid‑point) without making a new low.
5. **Entry Confirmation (1m/5m)**
    - On the retest of that FVG, you wait for:
        - A **bullish 1m or 5m candle close** that holds above/within the FVG and pushes away with clear body (not a doji).
    - No trade if price wicks through and closes weak or chops inside the FVG for >10–15 minutes.
6. **Risk & Target**
    - Stop: Below the MSS low or below the FVG low (structural invalidation).
    - Target: Next **internal high**, then session high / HTF buy‑side draw.

### A+ MSS example

- Daily shows price trading into a large bullish 4H FVG that also swept a prior weekly low (HTF draw to buy‑side above).
- London session:
    - Presses down and takes out **Asia Low** and yesterday’s low in one fast push (clear liquidity run).
- Right after the sweep:
    - On 5m, a strong bullish displacement candle tears higher, breaks above the last **5m lower high** and leaves a clean 5m FVG.
- NY Open:
    - Price retraces into that 5m FVG.
    - On 1m, you see a quick tap and then a **full‑body bullish candle closes back above the FVG midpoint** with almost no lower wick.
- You enter on that 1m close.
    - Stop goes a few ticks below the MSS low.
    - TP1 at the last internal high, TP2 at **London high**, and runner toward prior day high/HTF buy‑side.

This is A+ because **HTF draw, liquidity sweep, MSS, FVG, and confirmation candle** all align.

## Trend model – Continuation in the direction of displacement

**Purpose:** Join an already‑established move after the MSS is done and a clean trend is in play.

### Core components

For a **bullish Trend** model:

1. **Context & HTF Bias**
    - HTF (Daily/4H) is clearly moving higher, leaving a series of **bullish FVGs** that are being respected (price bounces from them repeatedly).[6]
    - NY or earlier session already produced a clear MSS to the upside; you are now in the continuation phase.[1]
2. **Strong Impulse Leg**
    - On 5m or 1m, price makes a **strong impulse leg up** (wide‑range candles, little overlap), again leaving one or more fresh bullish FVGs.[6]
3. **Pullback into Internal FVG**
    - Price begins to pull back **within the bullish structure**:
        - Highs/lows are still forming higher highs / higher lows.
        - Pullback respects structure and trades into an **internal bullish FVG** in the direction of the overall trend.[7][9]
4. **Entry Confirmation (1m/5m)**
    - As price hits the FVG:
        - You wait for a **bullish 1m or 5m close** that:
            - Closes above the FVG midpoint (not deep into it).
            - Shows displacement (body stronger than surrounding pullback candles).
    - No trade if:
        - Price chops sideways in the FVG.
        - Price breaks market structure down (no longer higher lows).
5. **Risk & Target**
    - Stop: Below the swing low that touches the FVG or below the FVG low itself.[9]
    - Target: Next internal high, then higher‑timeframe draw (e.g., yesterday’s high / 4H buy‑side).

### A+ Trend example

- Daily/4H: Market is in a sustained up‑move, respecting prior bullish 4H FVGs.[6]
- London created a huge displacement leg up, MSS to the upside, and left two bullish FVGs. NY is opening above them.
- During NY:
    - On 5m, price rallies again, making new highs and leaving a **fresh 5m bullish FVG** in the process.
    - It then retraces into that 5m FVG with small, orderly red candles—no aggressive selling.
- At the FVG:
    - 1m prints a small bottoming wick, then a **strong bullish candle closes above the FVG CE**.
- Entry:
    - You buy on that 1m close, place stop below the local swing low/FVG.
    - TP1: At the high that defined the pullback.
    - TP2: At the **next HTF buy‑side target** (e.g., prior daily high).

This is A+ because **primary trend, HTF FVG respect, clean impulse, clean pullback, and decisive confirmation** are all present.[9][6]

## Inversion model – Opposing FVG failure

**Purpose:** Trade the **failure** of an opposing PD array as additional confirmation of your bias. A bearish FVG failing in a bullish environment becomes a **bullish inversion FVG**, and vice versa.[10][11]

### Core components

For a **bullish Inversion**:

1. **Context & HTF Bias**
    - HTF is clearly bullish: strong upward displacement, buy‑side liquidity targets above, prior bearish PD arrays being consumed.[11][10]
    - You already expect higher prices.
2. **Opposing FVG forms**
    - On 5m/1m, within the up‑move, a **bearish FVG** appears (a small counter‑trend imbalance created during a short‑term sell‑off).[10][11]
3. **Violation of the Bearish FVG**
    - Instead of price respecting that bearish FVG and selling off:
        - Price **blasts through it**, and
        - A candle **closes above the top of that bearish FVG** (or above its midpoint with strong displacement).[12][10]
    - This transforms the failed bearish FVG into a **bullish inversion FVG**.
4. **Optional Retest & Confirmation**
    - Conservative approach:
        - Wait for price to pull back into the **inversion zone** (the violated FVG area) and then:
            - A 1m/5m **bullish candle closes back up** from that zone (respecting it now as support).[10]
    - Aggressive approach:
        - Enter on the initial close that violated the FVG, if overall context and speed are exceptional.[12]
5. **Risk & Target**
    - Stop: Below the inversion FVG low or below the candle that closed through it.[10]
    - Target: Next buy‑side liquidity (session high, PDH, HTF high).[11]

### A+ Inversion example

- HTF shows:
    - 4H bullish FVGs have been respected for days.
    - Price is approaching a prior weekly high (main buy‑side draw).[4][10]
- During NY:
    - After a strong rally, 5m prints a small **bearish FVG** as price dips slightly (micro pullback).
- Then:
    - On 5m, a large green candle rips **straight through that bearish FVG** and closes well above it, leaving no sign of rejection.[12][10]
    - On 1m, right after, price pulls back into the inversion zone and prints a **full‑body bullish candle** rejecting from that zone.
- Entry:
    - You buy on the 1m confirmation close in the inversion zone.
    - Stop under the inversion low.
    - TP: First at intraday high, then at the **weekly high** above (HTF draw).

This is A+ because a **counter‑trend PD array fails exactly in the direction of HTF bias**, and the inversion zone is defended with clear bullish confirmation.[11][10]

---

If you internalize these three patterns—MSS for turns, Trend for continuation, Inversion for failed opposing PD arrays—and only execute when the **A+ conditions** are met (HTF draw + Good Price + confirmation close), you will be trading very close to how Lanto structures his entries.
